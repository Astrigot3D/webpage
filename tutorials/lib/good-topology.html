A Practical Guide to 3D Model Topology in Blender

Intermediate
~30 min read
Published: April 27, 2025

Let's dive into the essentials of good 3D model topology and figure out how to actually *use* it in Blender, whether you're aiming for animation, games, or smooth subdivision modeling.

I. Let's Talk Topology: What It Is and Why You Should Care

A. Topology: More Than Just Wires

So, what's this "topology" everyone talks about in 3D? Simply put, topology is how your model's mesh is built – the way its vertices, edges, and faces are arranged and connected to create the final shape. Think of it like the underlying skeleton or blueprint that holds your model together. Every mesh is made of:

  • Vertices: These are just single points in 3D space. In Blender's Edit Mode, they look like dots.
  • Edges: These are the straight lines connecting two vertices. They form the 'wires' you see in wireframe view.
  • Faces: These are the flat surfaces filling the gaps between edges (usually triangles, quads, or sometimes more complex shapes called N-gons). These faces are what actually catch the light and become visible when you render.

It's easy to think topology is just about making the wireframe look neat, but it goes much deeper than that. A clean wireframe is often a *result* of good topology, but the real goal is creating a mesh structure that's both effective and efficient. Good topology means the mesh parts define the shape you want without wasting polygons, and they make the model easy to work with, whether you're just rendering it, trying to animate it, or planning to sculpt more detail.

B. Why Bother with Topology? (Hint: It Affects Everything)

You can't just throw polygons down randomly and expect good results. The way you arrange them has a massive impact on almost every stage of the 3D process. Getting a grip on why topology matters is the first step to making better models.

  • Efficiency: Smart topology uses polygons wisely. You put more detail (more polys) where you need it – like around curves or areas that will bend – and use fewer polys on big, flat, simple areas. A common beginner mistake is adding edge loops for a small detail, but letting those loops run across the entire model. This bloats the polygon count and can even mess up other areas. Good topology keeps detail localized, making your models lighter and easier to manage.
  • Deformation (Animation!): This is where topology *really* shows its importance, especially if you plan to animate your model. If your edge loops flow naturally along the shape, following muscle lines or the way a joint should bend, the model will squash, stretch, and bend realistically. Bad topology, where edges fight the direction of movement, leads to ugly kinks, geometry collapsing in on itself, and other nightmares during animation. It makes rigging and skinning (attaching the mesh to the skeleton) a *lot* harder.
  • Shading & Rendering: Believe it or not, topology affects how your model looks when lit. Light interacts with the surface based on *surface normals* (vectors pointing out from each face). How these normals get calculated and smoothed depends on the underlying polygon layout. Bad topology – think non-flat faces, weird poles (vertices where too many edges meet), or sudden jumps in polygon density – can mess up the smooth shading, causing pinching, dark spots, or weird highlights. You might blame your materials or textures, but the real culprit is often the mesh structure itself.
  • Workflow: Clean, logical topology just makes your life easier. UV unwrapping (flattening the model for texturing) is way less painful when edge loops flow predictably. Selecting parts of your mesh becomes simpler. And if you need to tweak the model later? It's much less of a headache with a well-organized mesh compared to wrestling with a tangled mess. Poor topology can even break some modeling tools – loop cuts might stop unexpectedly, or selections won't work right.

Beyond the technical stuff, good topology even helps communicate the form. When edge flow follows the 'anatomy' of an object, it reinforces the shape and guides the eye, making the structure clear even without textures. A mesh where edges run logically just *looks* right and makes sense. Confusing topology, on the other hand, can obscure the form and make it harder for collaborators (or your future self!) to understand and work with. So, thoughtful topology isn't just functional; it contributes to the model's clarity and readability.

II. The Mesh Ingredients: Verts, Edges, Faces, Poles & Loops

To really get topology, we need to understand the bits and pieces: vertices, edges, the faces they make (quads, tris, n-gons), and some key concepts like poles and loops that define the mesh's structure.

A. Quads, Tris, and N-gons: Your Polygon Toolkit

The faces making up your model's surface usually fall into three main categories based on their number of sides:

  • Triangles (Tris): Three-sided polygons. They're the simplest possible face and have the neat property of always being perfectly flat (planar). Graphics cards ultimately think in triangles, so they're fundamental for real-time stuff like games.
  • Quadrilaterals (Quads): Four-sided polygons. These are often the go-to choice for modeling, especially for organic shapes or anything you plan to subdivide or animate. Their structure makes creating clean edge loops easier, and they generally deform and subdivide more predictably.
  • N-gons: Any polygon with more than four sides (5, 6, 7...). N-gons can be handy during modeling, maybe for quickly capping a hole or dealing with the results of a boolean operation. However, they often need careful cleanup later because they can cause headaches with subdivision, animation, and shading, and aren't always well-supported everywhere.

B. Poles: Where Edges Converge

Let's talk about poles. A pole is simply a vertex where the number of connecting edges isn't the usual four you'd find in a perfect grid of quads. Understanding poles is vital because where they sit can really affect how smooth your surface looks and how it deforms. You'll mainly encounter:

  • N-pole (or 3-pole): A vertex where exactly three edges meet. These pop up naturally at corners or when you intentionally redirect edge flow (like at the base of a character's nose).
  • E-pole (or 5+ pole): A vertex where five or more edges come together. These often appear after operations like extruding faces or where several edge loops crash into each other.

Poles aren't inherently evil – you pretty much *need* them to model anything complex or to change the direction of edge flow. But they, especially E-poles, are known trouble spots. They can cause pinching, stretching, or other weird artifacts when you subdivide the mesh or try to animate it. That's why a big part of good topology is controlling *where* you place poles and trying to avoid unnecessary E-poles.

C. Loops: Defining Form and Flow

Loops are your friends. They provide structure and help control how the mesh behaves, especially when it needs to bend or deform.

  • Edge Loops: Imagine a continuous path of connected edges flowing across your model's surface, often following its contours. A proper edge loop connects vertices where (usually) each vertex in the middle of the loop connects to exactly two other vertices *outside* the loop, one on each side. For organic models, edge loops are king. You want them to follow muscle forms or wrap around joints (knees, elbows) and facial features (eyes, mouth) so the model deforms naturally. Blender makes selecting them easy (usually Alt+Click on an edge).
  • Face Loops: These are simply the strips of faces lying between two adjacent edge loops. Handy for selecting bands of polygons.

Well-planned edge and face loops are the foundation of clean "edge flow" (which we'll get to next). They make subdivision modeling work beautifully and are essential for character animation. They give your mesh a predictable, controllable structure.

III. The Golden Rules of Good Topology

While the "perfect" topology depends on the job, some core principles generally lead to meshes that work better, look better, and are easier to handle. Aiming for these will usually steer you right.

A. Keep Your Edge Flow Clean

Edge flow is all about the direction those edge loops take as they run across the mesh surface. Good topology aims for a flow that's clean, makes sense, and supports the model's shape.

Ideally, your edges should flow along the natural curves and structural lines of whatever you're modeling. For a character, loops might follow muscle shapes or wrap around the eyes and mouth. For a hard-surface object, they might define the main forms or run parallel to sharp edges or bevels. Why? This helps define the shape accurately, makes shading look smoother because polygons align with the surface curves, and—crucially for animation—allows the model to bend predictably along natural lines. Messy edge flow that ignores the form can create weird visual bumps or dips, mess up shading, and make the intended shape harder to read.

B. Consistent Polygon Size (Mostly!)

Generally, it's good practice to aim for polygons that are roughly the same size and spacing across your surface. This consistency helps create smoother surfaces, makes subdivision results more predictable, and avoids shading problems caused by sudden shifts in mesh resolution. Try to avoid huge, stretched-out polygons next to tiny ones if you can help it.

However, keeping the density uniform *everywhere* is often wasteful. The real trick is to vary polygon density based on need. Areas that need lots of detail (like faces, hands, intricate patterns) or areas that need to deform a lot (like joints) should have higher density (more, smaller polygons) to capture the complexity or allow smooth bending. Big, flat, or simple areas can get away with much lower density (fewer, larger polygons), which saves processing power and memory.

Watch out for this common trap: adding edge loops for detail in one spot, but letting them run across the whole model, adding polygons everywhere unnecessarily. Good topology techniques involve terminating these loops cleverly, keeping the detail (and density) localized only where it's actually doing something useful.

C. Tame Your Poles: Minimize and Place Wisely

We know poles (vertices with not-quite-four edges) are often necessary but can cause pinching or shading issues, especially those E-poles (5+ edges) when subdivided or deformed.

The goal isn't to eliminate *every* pole – that's often impossible on complex shapes. Instead, focus on getting rid of *unnecessary* ones and being smart about where the *necessary* ones go. A common tactic is to push unavoidable poles onto flatter parts of the mesh where the surface isn't curving much, or into areas that won't deform much during animation, or even just hide them where the camera is less likely to see them. Strategic pole placement lets you redirect edge flow cleanly while minimizing the ugly side effects.

D. Keep it "Watertight" (Manifold)

Think of manifold geometry as a mesh that could theoretically hold water – no holes, no weird internal faces, no edges connecting more than two faces. Every edge should connect exactly two faces, and every vertex should be part of a boundary. Non-manifold geometry includes things like edges shared by three or more faces, floating vertices not connected to anything, or faces pointing the wrong way (flipped normals).

Why care? Because most tools and processes downstream expect manifold geometry. Subdivision surface modifiers, physics simulations, 3D printing, and even some renderers can throw errors, behave unpredictably, or just fail completely if they encounter non-manifold issues. Luckily, Blender has built-in tools to find these problems (Select > Select All by Trait > Non Manifold) so you can fix them.

It's a Balancing Act

Mastering topology means understanding how these principles interact. Adding loops for detail affects edge flow. Stopping loops often creates poles. Changing edge flow might mean adjusting polygon density. There's rarely one single "right" answer; it's about making smart trade-offs to best serve your model's purpose.

IV. Quads vs. Tris vs. N-gons: The Great Debate

Choosing between quads (4 sides), tris (3 sides), and N-gons (more than 4 sides) is a constant discussion in 3D modeling. Each has its strengths and weaknesses, and the best choice really depends on what you're doing.

A. The Breakdown: Pros and Cons

  • Quads (4 Sides):
    • The Good: They subdivide cleanly and predictably (great for smooth surfaces), deform nicely (essential for animation), make edge loop selection easy, and generally lead to smoother shading. They're the workhorse for organic modeling and SubD workflows.
    • The Catch: Sometimes forcing everything to be a quad can lead to overly complex or inefficient topology, especially on very flat or very simple areas.
  • Tris (3 Sides):
    • The Good: Always perfectly flat, they are the native language of game engines and renderers, excellent for terminating edge loops neatly, and efficient on flat surfaces that won't deform or subdivide. A must-know for game art.
    • The Catch: They interrupt smooth edge flow, can cause pinching artifacts when subdivided, and stop edge loop selections dead in their tracks.
  • N-gons (>4 Sides):
    • The Good: Can speed up modeling on large flat surfaces (fewer clicks!), useful as a temporary stage (like after a boolean), and can sometimes reduce polycount on perfectly flat, non-deforming areas.
    • The Catch: Generally behave badly with subdivision and deformation, prone to shading errors (especially if not perfectly flat), can cause compatibility issues with other software or steps in the pipeline, and how they get triangulated internally can be ambiguous. Handle with care!

B. When Quads Are Your Best Bet

Think of quads as your default, especially for anything that needs to deform (animation) or will be smoothed using a Subdivision Surface modifier. Their predictable behavior under stress and natural partnership with edge loops make them ideal for rigging, smooth movement, and getting clean results from SubD workflows.

C. When Tris Are Okay (or Even Necessary)

Don't be afraid of tris! They're essential for real-time applications like games where triangle count is king and everything gets turned into tris eventually anyway. They're also perfectly fine on flat surfaces that won't bend or get subdivided. You can even use them strategically to terminate an edge loop or redirect flow, often creating a manageable 3-edge pole (N-pole) in the process.

D. N-gons: Use Sparingly and Wisely

N-gons have their place, mostly as a modeling shortcut (e.g., cleaning up after booleans) or for representing large, perfectly flat, static surfaces in hard-surface modeling. But remember their downsides: unpredictable subdivision, potential shading glitches (especially if not flat), and export/compatibility risks. The general advice? Clean them up (convert to quads or tris) in your final model unless they sit on a perfectly flat area that won't deform, won't be subdivided, and won't cause issues downstream.

Forget "Quad Purity" - Think Purpose

You'll hear debates about making everything quads ("quad purity"). While quads are often preferred, being too rigid about it can be counterproductive. Experienced modelers know when a well-placed tri or even an N-gon can solve a problem effectively. Focus on what works best for the specific situation and the final goal, not just dogma.

V. Different Jobs, Different Topology Needs

What counts as "good" topology really hinges on what you're going to *do* with the model. The ideal setup for animation is quite different from what you need for a game asset or a base mesh for sculpting.

A. Animation: Building for Movement

If your model needs to bend, twist, or deform, topology is paramount. Edge loops absolutely *must* follow the paths where movement will happen. Joints like knees and elbows need specific loop structures (you might hear about the "three-loop rule") to stop them from collapsing or pinching when bent and to help maintain their volume. Facial animation? That relies heavily on clean loops circling the eyes and mouth. Areas that deform need enough quad density to make the transitions look smooth, not blocky. Good topology here makes the challenging jobs of rigging and skinning much, much easier.

B. Real-Time (Games): Lean and Mean

Game topology is all about performance, which means low polygon counts (usually measured in tris). Every single polygon has to justify its existence. Waste is bad! Models are often explicitly triangulated before being exported to the game engine to ensure consistency. N-gons are almost always a no-go. A clean base topology also makes creating simplified versions (Level of Detail or LOD models) easier. Furthermore, the topology needs to allow for clean UV unwrapping, as details are often faked using normal maps baked from a high-poly version.

C. Subdivision Modeling: The Control Cage

Working with subdivision surface modifiers (like Blender's) requires a clean, mostly quad-based "control cage." N-gons will cause weird smoothing artifacts, and while tris can be used, they can cause pinching if not placed carefully on flat areas. Controlling sharpness is key: you either add extra "holding" or "support" edge loops very close to the edge you want to keep sharp (this adds geometry), or you use edge creasing (which keeps the cage cleaner but can sometimes look a bit harsher). Placing any necessary poles strategically on flatter areas is vital to avoid pinching on the final smooth surface.

D. Sculpting: Setting Up the Canvas

Often, the best starting point for digital sculpting is a base mesh made of evenly distributed quads. This provides a uniform "digital clay" to work on. Tools like Blender's Dyntopo (Dynamic Topology) let you sculpt freely without worrying about the initial topology (it adds detail only where you sculpt), but the resulting mesh is usually quite messy. This sculpt then needs "retopology"—creating a brand new, clean mesh over the detailed sculpt—before it can be properly textured, animated, or used in many other workflows.

E. Hard-Surface vs. Organic: Different Strategies

  • Organic: The focus here is usually on smooth, flowing edge loops that follow the curves and forms of the subject (like muscles, wrinkles). Quads are generally preferred, especially if deformation or subdivision is planned. Careful placement of poles to redirect flow without causing problems is crucial.
  • Hard-Surface: This deals more with sharp edges, flat planes, and precise shapes. N-gons and tris might be perfectly fine on flat areas that won't deform or get subdivided. Boolean operations are common tools, but they often create messy topology that needs cleaning up afterwards. Controlling edge sharpness using bevels, support loops, or creasing is a major consideration.

VI. Your Topology Tools in Blender

Blender comes packed with great tools to help you build and refine your mesh topology. Knowing your toolkit is half the battle.

A. Everyday Mesh Editing (Edit Mode Essentials)

These are your bread-and-butter tools for shaping topology:

  • Loop Cut (Ctrl+R): Adds new edge loops – fundamental for adding detail and controlling flow.
  • Inset Faces (I): Creates smaller faces inside selected ones, great for panels or adding support loops quickly.
  • Extrude (E): Pulls out new geometry from selected vertices, edges, or faces.
  • Knife Tool (K): Lets you draw new edges freely across faces – use with care, but powerful for redirects or complex cuts.
  • Vertex/Edge Slide (G, G): Slides selected elements along their connected edges without changing the overall form much. Great for tidying up loops.
  • Bevel (Ctrl+B): Creates chamfered or rounded corners/edges. Essential for hard-surface and controlling sharpness for subdivision.
  • Merge Vertices (M): Combines selected vertices – key for joining parts, simplifying, and terminating loops.
  • Fill (F) / Grid Fill: Tools for creating faces to close holes. Grid Fill is particularly good at creating clean quad topology in openings.

B. Retopology: Building a New Skin

When you have a dense sculpt or messy model, you often need to rebuild its topology:

  • Manual Retopology: This is the classic approach. You create a new mesh, often using snapping tools (like Face Snapping) combined with basic modeling tools (Extrude, Fill) and maybe the Shrinkwrap modifier, to essentially draw the new, clean topology directly onto the surface of the high-poly source mesh. It gives you the most control but takes time.
  • Add-ons and Automated Tools: Blender has helpful add-ons (like the built-in F2 for filling quads) and there are powerful commercial options (like Quad Remesher) that can automate much of the retopology process. These are huge time-savers, but you'll often still need to do some manual cleanup for the best results, especially for animation.

C. Modifiers to the Rescue

Modifiers work non-destructively and are topology powerhouses:

  • Subdivision Surface: The magic behind smooth surfaces from low-poly cages. Its behavior is entirely dependent on your base topology.
  • Bevel Modifier: Applies bevels without committing the geometry, often used with edge bevel weights for precise control in hard-surface modeling.
  • Mirror Modifier: Essential for working symmetrically, ensuring your topology is mirrored perfectly across an axis.
  • Shrinkwrap Modifier: Makes one mesh conform to the surface of another – the backbone of many manual retopology workflows.
  • Boolean Modifier: Cuts shapes out of or joins shapes together. Powerful, but often leaves N-gons and messy topology that needs manual cleanup afterwards.

D. Select Smarter, Not Harder

Good topology makes selection easier, and Blender has tools to leverage that:

  • Loop Select (Alt+Click Edge): Instantly selects an entire edge loop. Indispensable.
  • Ring Select (Ctrl+Alt+Click Edge): Selects a ring of parallel edges.
  • Select Linked (L / Ctrl+L): Selects all connected geometry under the cursor or from the current selection.
  • Select More/Less (Ctrl+NumPad +/-): Expands or shrinks your selection based on topological connection.
  • Select Similar (Shift+G): Selects geometry based on shared properties (like normals, number of connecting edges, etc.).
  • Select Faces by Sides: Lets you quickly select all tris, quads, or N-gons in your mesh for inspection or cleanup.

VII. Topology in the Wild: Quick Examples

Let's look at how these topology ideas play out in a few common situations.

A. Example 1: Bending an Arm (Elbow/Knee)

Bad Topology: Edge loops run straight down the arm/leg. When the joint bends, the geometry collapses unnaturally, losing volume and creating sharp kinks. Good Topology: Edge loops flow *around* the joint, following the crease. Additional support loops are placed parallel on either side of the main bend line. This structure helps maintain the shape and volume for a much more natural-looking bend.

B. Example 2: A Smooth Panel with a Hole (Subdivision Surface)

Bad Topology: Using N-gons or random tris near the curved edges or around the hole causes pinching, bumps, or shading errors when the Subdivision modifier is applied. Poorly placed edge loops might create ripples across the smooth surface. Good Topology: The main curved surfaces use clean quad flow. The hole is integrated smoothly, perhaps using quads that radiate outwards or carefully placed tris only on the perfectly flat sections away from curves. Sharp edges around the panel or hole are defined using tight support loops or edge creasing, resulting in a clean, predictable smoothed shape.

C. Example 3: Optimizing a Prop for a Game

The Process: You might start modeling with quads and maybe even subdivision for smooth shapes. Then, the optimization begins: remove edge loops that don't contribute significantly to the silhouette or deformation. Strategically convert quads to tris on flat or hidden areas to save polygons. Keep a close eye on the target triangle count (the "budget"). Before exporting, manually triangulate the mesh (or use a tool) to ensure it looks the same in the engine as it does in Blender. Optimize the UV layout for efficient texturing, possibly baking details from a high-poly version onto a normal map for the low-poly game model. The final topology prioritizes silhouette, UVs, and staying within the triangle budget.

VIII. The Takeaway: "Good" Topology Depends on the Goal

Ultimately, there's no single definition of "perfect" topology. What works brilliantly for one purpose might be terrible for another. It's all about context.

A. Putting It All Together: Making Smart Choices

Getting good at topology means internalizing the core principles—edge flow, density, poles, manifold geometry—and then applying them thoughtfully based on the specific needs of your project:

  • Will it animate or deform significantly?
  • Will it use subdivision surfaces?
  • Is it for a game engine or offline rendering?
  • Are there strict performance limits (poly counts)?
  • What modeling techniques are involved (sculpting, booleans)?
  • Is it an organic shape or a hard-surface object?

Apply the rules where they matter most. Don't over-engineer the topology for a simple, static background object that will never deform or be seen up close.

B. Quick Tips for Blender Users

Here's a distilled version of the advice:

  1. Start with Quads: They offer the most flexibility initially.
  2. Focus on Edge Flow: Make loops follow the form and structure.
  3. Manage Density: Add detail locally; terminate loops cleanly to avoid unnecessary polys.
  4. Handle Poles Carefully: Minimize E-poles (5+ edges); place necessary poles where they won't cause trouble (flat, non-deforming areas).
  5. Use Tris Deliberately: Great for games, truly flat static areas, and ending loops cleanly.
  6. Be Wary of N-gons: Best saved for large flat static areas or as temporary geometry that needs cleanup.
  7. Know Your Tools: Get comfortable with Blender's modeling, selection, modifier, and retopology features.
  8. Learn by Looking: Analyze topology in professional models. Ask *why* they made certain choices.
  9. Practice, Practice, Practice: There's no substitute for experience. Build things!

By understanding these principles, mastering Blender's tools, and always considering your model's final purpose, you'll be well on your way to creating cleaner, more efficient, and more functional 3D models.

Answer Key

1. Define topology...

Topology is basically how a 3D mesh is constructed – the pattern of how its vertices, edges, and faces are connected to form the object's shape. It's the underlying structure or framework.

2. Why is good topology important for animation?

For animation, good topology is key because edge loops that follow the natural flow of movement allow the model to bend and deform smoothly and realistically, without ugly pinching or collapsing geometry.

3. What is an N-gon...?

An N-gon is just a face with more than four sides. While often problematic, you might use them temporarily after a boolean operation before cleaning up, or sometimes leave them on large, perfectly flat areas that won't be subdivided or deformed.

4. Explain edge loops...

An edge loop is a continuous path of connected edges running along the model's surface, usually following its contours. For organic models, they're vital because aligning them with muscles or joints helps the model deform naturally and gives you control over the shape.

5. What is a pole...?

A pole is a vertex where more or fewer than four edges meet. You need to be careful placing them, especially E-poles (5+ edges), because they can cause pinching or shading artifacts when the mesh is subdivided or deformed.

6. Why are quads preferred for subdivision?

Quads are generally preferred for subdivision because common algorithms (like Catmull-Clark in Blender) are designed to subdivide them very predictably into four smaller quads, leading to smooth, controllable surfaces without easily causing weird artifacts.

7. When are tris necessary?

Tris are often necessary, especially for game development, since game engines render in triangles anyway, and triangulating beforehand ensures consistency. They're also fine on perfectly flat areas or when used strategically to terminate edge loops or redirect flow.

8. What is manifold geometry?

Manifold geometry means the mesh is "watertight" like a real-world object – no holes, every edge connects exactly two faces. It's important because many tools (like subdivision, simulation, 3D printing) expect this clean structure and can fail or produce errors with non-manifold meshes.

9. Explain holding edges...

Holding edges (or support loops) are extra edge loops placed very close and parallel to an edge you want to keep sharp. When subdivided, the smoothing effect gets concentrated in the tiny space between the loops, effectively preserving the sharpness of the original edge.

10. Difference between organic and hard-surface topology?

A key difference is edge flow emphasis: organic models prioritize smooth, flowing loops following natural forms for deformation (usually quad-heavy). Hard-surface often focuses on defining sharp edges and flat planes, potentially using tris/N-gons more freely on flat, static areas, and relying heavily on techniques like bevels or creasing to control sharpness.

Key Terms Glossary

Topology:

How a 3D mesh's vertices, edges, and faces are connected to form its shape.

Mesh:

The collection of vertices, edges, and faces that make up a 3D object.

Vertex (Vertices):

A single point in 3D space; a corner of a polygon.

Edge:

A line segment connecting two vertices.

Face:

A flat surface defined by connecting edges (making a polygon).

Polygon:

A face on the mesh, typically a Tri, Quad, or N-gon.

Triangle (Tri):

A three-sided polygon. Always flat, fundamental for rendering.

Quadrilateral (Quad):

A four-sided polygon. Often preferred for subdivision and animation.

N-gon:

A polygon with more than four sides. Use with caution.

Pole:

A vertex where something other than 4 edges meet.

N-pole (3-pole):

A pole where exactly 3 edges meet.

E-pole (5+ pole):

A pole where 5 or more edges meet. Often causes issues.

Edge Loop:

A continuous path of connected edges, usually following the surface flow.

Face Loop:

A strip of connected faces, typically lying between two edge loops.

Edge Flow:

The visual direction and pathways created by edge loops across the mesh.

Polygon Density:

How closely packed polygons are in different areas of the mesh.

Manifold Geometry:

A mesh that's "watertight" with no gaps or weird connections (like edges shared by 3+ faces).

Non-manifold Geometry:

Mesh errors like holes, internal faces, or edges with more than two connected faces.

Subdivision Surface Modifier:

A Blender modifier that smooths a mesh by adding more polygons based on the original cage.

Holding Edges (Support Loops):

Extra edge loops placed near an edge to keep it sharp during subdivision.

Edge Creasing:

A Blender property assigned to edges to control their sharpness under subdivision without adding geometry.

Retopology:

The process of building a new, cleaner mesh structure over an existing detailed or messy one.

UV Unwrapping:

The process of flattening a 3D mesh's surface into a 2D map for applying textures.

Normal Map:

A special texture that fakes surface detail on a low-poly model by manipulating how light reflects off it.

Planar:

Describes something that lies perfectly flat on a single plane.

Deformation:

The way a mesh changes shape, especially when being bent or moved during animation.
